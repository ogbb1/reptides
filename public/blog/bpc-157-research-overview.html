BPC-157 Research Overview: Mechanism of Action, Molecular Structure, and Purity Specifications

Published April 2026 · 7 min read · For in-vitro research use only

BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound-157) is a synthetic pentadecapeptide consisting of 15 amino acids. It is a partial sequence derived from a protein found in human gastric juice. BPC-157 is one of the most widely studied research peptides in tissue biology, with over 100 published studies examining its activity in various in-vitro and preclinical models.

This article provides a research-focused overview of BPC-157 including its molecular structure, known signaling pathways, stability characteristics, purity requirements, and applications in laboratory research. All information is for research reference only.

Mechanism of Action: Known Signaling Pathways

BPC-157 research has identified multiple signaling pathways through which the peptide exerts biological activity in laboratory models. The following pathways have been described in published in-vitro and preclinical studies:

Nitric Oxide (NO) System

BPC-157 has been studied for its interaction with the nitric oxide system. In vitro research suggests BPC-157 may modulate NO synthesis through effects on endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) expression. Published studies have examined BPC-157 in NO-mediated vascular function assays and endothelial cell culture models.

Growth Factor Expression

Multiple studies have investigated BPC-157 in the context of growth factor signaling. Published research includes studies examining VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor), EGF (epidermal growth factor), and FGF (fibroblast growth factor) expression in cell culture models treated with BPC-157.

FAK-Paxillin Pathway

Research has identified BPC-157 activity in the focal adhesion kinase (FAK) and paxillin signaling cascade, which is involved in cell migration, adhesion, and extracellular matrix interaction. These studies use in-vitro wound healing assays and cell migration models.

Dopaminergic System

BPC-157 has been studied in dopamine receptor signaling research, with published literature examining its activity in dopamine D1 and D2 receptor systems. These studies are conducted in preclinical neurological research models.
