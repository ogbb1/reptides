Epithalon and MOTS-c: Longevity and Epigenetic Peptide Research

Published April 6, 2026 · 7 min read

Epithalon and MOTS-c represent two distinct approaches to longevity and cellular aging research. Epithalon targets nuclear telomere maintenance through telomerase activation, while MOTS-c operates as a mitochondrial-derived signaling peptide activating metabolic stress response pathways.

Epithalon (Epitalon): Telomerase Research Peptide

Epithalon (also spelled Epitalon) is a synthetic tetrapeptide with the sequence Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly. It was developed based on the Epithalamin extract from the pineal gland. The primary research interest in Epithalon centers on its interaction with telomerase, the ribonucleoprotein enzyme complex that maintains telomere length by adding TTAGGG repeat sequences to chromosome ends.

Telomere shortening is a fundamental mechanism of cellular aging. Each cell division results in the loss of 50-200 base pairs from telomeric DNA due to the end-replication problem. When telomeres reach a critical length, cells enter replicative senescence or undergo apoptosis. Telomerase activity, primarily through the catalytic subunit hTERT (human Telomerase Reverse Transcriptase), can counteract this shortening.

Research demonstrates that Epithalon treatment in cell culture models increases hTERT gene expression and telomerase enzymatic activity as measured by TRAP (Telomeric Repeat Amplification Protocol) assays. Studies have also examined Epithalon's effects on melatonin secretion patterns and circadian rhythm gene expression.

MOTS-c: Mitochondrial-Derived Peptide

MOTS-c (Mitochondrial Open reading frame of the Twelve S rRNA type-c) is a 16 amino acid peptide encoded within the mitochondrial 12S rRNA gene. It is the first mitochondrial-derived peptide shown to have systemic metabolic signaling functions, acting as a retrograde signal from mitochondria to the nucleus.

MOTS-c activates the AMPK (AMP-activated Protein Kinase) pathway, the cellular energy sensor that regulates metabolic homeostasis. AMPK activation triggers a cascade of metabolic adaptations including enhanced glucose uptake, fatty acid oxidation, and mitochondrial biogenesis. MOTS-c also modulates the folate-methionine cycle, linking mitochondrial signaling to epigenetic regulation through one-carbon metabolism.

Research applications include AMPK pathway characterization, mitochondrial-nuclear crosstalk signaling studies, metabolic stress response modeling, and comparative analysis with other mitochondrial-derived peptides (Humanin, SHLPs).

NAD+ and Sirtuin Connection

Both Epithalon and MOTS-c research intersect with the NAD+/sirtuin axis. NAD+ (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide) is an essential coenzyme that serves as a substrate for sirtuins (SIRT1-7), NAD+-dependent deacetylases that regulate gene expression, DNA repair, and metabolic function. NAD+ supplementation research examines how restoring cellular NAD+ levels affects sirtuin activity and downstream longevity-associated pathways.
