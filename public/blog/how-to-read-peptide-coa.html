How to Read a Peptide Certificate of Analysis (COA)

Published April 2026 · 6 min read · For in-vitro research use only

A Certificate of Analysis is the single most important document for verifying research peptide quality. It provides analytical data proving purity, molecular identity, and safety. If your peptide vendor does not provide a COA, or if their COA lacks key data, you have no way to verify what is in the vial.

This guide explains every section of a peptide COA so you know exactly what to look for and what the numbers mean.

1. HPLC Purity: The Most Important Number

HPLC stands for High-Performance Liquid Chromatography. It is the standard method for measuring peptide purity. The HPLC purity percentage tells you how much of the sample is the target peptide versus impurities, degradation products, or synthesis byproducts.

How to read HPLC purity

99%+ purity - Research grade. Suitable for reliable, reproducible in-vitro assays. This is what you want.

98-99% purity - Acceptable for most research applications. Minor impurity levels unlikely to affect most assays.

95-97% purity - Marginal. Impurity levels may introduce confounding variables in sensitive assays. Not recommended for quantitative research.

Below 95% - Not suitable for research. High impurity levels compromise data integrity.

Every product at Reptides.co is verified at 99% or higher HPLC purity. If a batch tests below 99%, we do not sell it.

2. The HPLC Chromatogram: Visual Proof of Purity

The chromatogram is the graph that accompanies the HPLC purity number. It is arguably more informative than the purity percentage alone. The chromatogram shows the detector response over time as the sample passes through the HPLC column.

What a good chromatogram looks like

One tall, sharp dominant peak - This is the target peptide. A single clean peak with minimal baseline noise indicates high purity.

Flat baseline - The area around the main peak should be flat with no significant secondary peaks. A flat baseline means minimal impurities.

Symmetric peak shape - The main peak should be roughly symmetric. Tailing or fronting can indicate column issues or sample degradation.

Red flags on a chromatogram

Multiple peaks of similar height - Indicates significant impurities or a mixture of compounds. The sample may not be what it claims to be.

Noisy baseline - High baseline noise can indicate contamination, degradation, or poor analytical technique.

No chromatogram provided - A COA that lists a purity percentage without the chromatogram image cannot be independently verified. The number alone is meaningless without the visual data.

This is the most common issue in the research peptide industry. Many vendors provide a purity number with no chromatogram. Without the chromatogram, you are trusting a number on a piece of paper with no supporting evidence. Always demand the chromatogram.

3. Mass Spectrometry: Confirming Molecular Identity

Mass spectrometry (MS) confirms that the peptide in the vial is actually the correct compound. While HPLC tells you how pure the sample is, mass spectrometry tells you what the sample is.

How to read mass spec data

The COA should list an observed molecular weight (or m/z value) and compare it to the theoretical molecular weight of the target peptide. These values should match within acceptable tolerance, typically within 0.1% or 1 dalton.

Example for BPC-157:

Theoretical MW: 1419.53 g/mol

Observed MW: 1419.51 g/mol

Match confirmed - The compound is BPC-157.

If the observed molecular weight does not match the theoretical weight, the sample may be a different peptide, a degradation product, or an incorrect synthesis. This is a critical quality check that many buyers overlook.

4. Endotoxin Testing (LAL Test)

The Limulus Amebocyte Lysate (LAL) test measures bacterial endotoxin contamination. Endotoxins are lipopolysaccharide fragments from gram-negative bacteria that can trigger inflammatory responses in biological research models, confounding experimental results.

Acceptable endotoxin levels

Less than 0.05 EU/mg - Standard research grade. Suitable for cell culture and in-vitro assays.

Less than 0.25 EU/mg - Acceptable for most applications.

No endotoxin data provided - You cannot verify the sample is free from bacterial contamination. This is especially problematic for cell culture research where endotoxin contamination can invalidate entire experiments.

5. Sterility Testing (USP 71)

USP 71 sterility testing confirms the absence of viable microorganisms. The COA should state "Negative for Growth" or "Pass" under sterility testing. This confirms the sample was manufactured and handled under sterile conditions.

6. Appearance and Physical Properties

The COA may also describe the physical appearance of the lyophilized product. For most research peptides, the expected appearance is a white to off-white lyophilized powder or cake. Significant discoloration (yellow, brown) may indicate oxidative degradation.

COA Red Flags: What to Watch For

  • Purity number with no chromatogram image. The number alone proves nothing.
  • No mass spectrometry data. You cannot confirm molecular identity without it.
  • COA is a generic template with no batch number or date. May be fabricated or reused across batches.
  • COA only available "upon request" or "after purchase." Legitimate vendors publish COAs before you buy.
  • Batch date is years old. Peptides degrade over time. A COA from 2023 does not validate a product sold in 2026.
  • Vendor refuses to provide raw chromatogram data. This is the single biggest red flag in the peptide industry.
