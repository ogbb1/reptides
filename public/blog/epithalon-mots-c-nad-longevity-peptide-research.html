Epithalon, MOTS-c, and NAD+: Longevity-Associated Research Compounds

Published by Reptides.co Research Team · For in-vitro research reference only

Epithalon, MOTS-c, and NAD+ represent three distinct biochemical approaches to studying cellular aging mechanisms. Each targets a different hallmark of aging: telomere maintenance, mitochondrial signaling, and NAD+-dependent enzyme activity respectively.

Epithalon (Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly)

Epithalon is a synthetic tetrapeptide based on epithalamine, a pineal gland extract studied by Professor Vladimir Khavinson at the Saint Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology. Molecular formula: C₁₄H₂₂N₄O₉, molecular weight approximately 390.3 Da.

In-vitro studies have investigated Epithalon's effects on telomerase — the enzyme responsible for maintaining telomere length at chromosome ends. Published cell culture data reports telomerase activation in human fetal fibroblast and neonatal fibroblast models, with treated cells achieving additional population doublings beyond the Hayflick limit observed in control cultures. Available as lyophilized powder in 10mg and 50mg research quantities.

MOTS-c (Mitochondrial ORF of 12S rRNA Type-C)

MOTS-c is a 16 amino acid peptide encoded within the mitochondrial genome — specifically within the 12S rRNA gene. This mitochondrial origin is significant because it represents a class of signaling molecules (mitochondrial-derived peptides) that originate from organellar DNA rather than nuclear DNA.

Cell culture and animal model studies have examined MOTS-c's interaction with the AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) pathway, a central metabolic sensor that regulates cellular energy homeostasis. AMPK activation is associated with metabolic adaptation responses in various cell types. Available in 10mg, 15mg, 20mg, and 40mg research quantities.

NAD+ (Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide)

NAD+ is a coenzyme found in all living cells, functioning as an electron carrier in mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and as an essential substrate for sirtuin enzymes (SIRT1-7) and poly(ADP-ribose) polymerases (PARPs). Intracellular NAD+ levels decline measurably with age in both rodent models and human tissue analyses.

Research into NAD+ supplementation in cell culture examines whether exogenous NAD+ can restore sirtuin activity, improve mitochondrial membrane potential, and reduce markers of cellular senescence. Available in 100mg, 500mg, and 1000mg research quantities.
