How to Read an HPLC Chromatogram for Research Peptides

Published by Reptides.co Research Team · For in-vitro research reference only

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is the standard analytical method for assessing peptide purity. Understanding how to read a chromatogram is essential for evaluating compound quality before use in research protocols.

What an HPLC Chromatogram Shows

A chromatogram is a plot of detector response (usually UV absorbance at 220nm) versus retention time (minutes). The sample is dissolved and passed through a column packed with stationary phase material. Different compounds interact differently with the column, causing them to elute (emerge) at different times. Each compound appears as a peak on the chromatogram.

Key Elements to Evaluate

Main Peak

The largest peak represents the target peptide. Its retention time should be consistent with the expected hydrophobicity of the compound. The area under this peak relative to total peak area gives the purity percentage. For research-grade peptides, this should be 98%+ with premium grades at 99%+.

Minor Peaks

Smaller peaks represent impurities — typically synthesis byproducts such as deletion sequences (missing one amino acid), truncated sequences, deamidated forms, or oxidized variants. Their combined area determines the impurity percentage. Well-resolved minor peaks indicate a clean separation and reliable purity measurement.

Baseline

A flat, stable baseline indicates good chromatographic conditions. Baseline drift, noise, or broad humps can indicate column degradation, mobile phase issues, or co-eluting impurities that aren't being resolved into distinct peaks — any of which reduce confidence in the reported purity value.

Peak Shape

Symmetrical, sharp peaks indicate a single pure compound eluting at that retention time. Tailing (asymmetric peaks with extended right side), fronting (extended left side), or shouldering (partially resolved overlap) suggest potential co-elution of closely related impurities with the main compound.

Method Parameters to Check

A complete HPLC report should specify the column type (typically C18 reverse phase), mobile phase composition (acetonitrile/water gradient with TFA modifier), flow rate, detection wavelength (220nm for peptide bonds), injection volume, and column temperature. These parameters affect retention times and resolution — results from different methods are not directly comparable.

Supplier COA vs Third-Party COA

A supplier-generated COA uses the manufacturer's own equipment and methods. Third-party COAs from independent laboratories like Janoshik Analytical provide external verification using separate equipment and methodology. Both are valuable — supplier COAs confirm manufacturing consistency while third-party COAs provide independent verification free from potential conflicts of interest.
