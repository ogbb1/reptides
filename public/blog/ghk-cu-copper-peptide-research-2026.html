GHK-Cu Copper Peptide: Research Overview, Structure, and Mechanism

Published by Reptides.co Research Team · For in-vitro research reference only

GHK-Cu (glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine copper) is a naturally occurring copper-binding tripeptide first isolated from human plasma in 1973 by Dr. Loren Pickart. It has become one of the fastest-growing research compounds in the peptide space, with search interest increasing over 1,000% year-over-year as of early 2026.

Molecular Structure

GHK-Cu consists of three amino acids — glycine, histidine, and lysine — coordinated with a copper(II) ion. Its molecular formula is C₁₄H₂₄N₆O₄·Cu²⁺ with a molecular weight of approximately 403.9 Da. The copper ion is chelated by the nitrogen atoms of the histidine imidazole ring and the glycine amino terminus, forming a stable complex at physiological pH.

Presence in Biological Systems

GHK-Cu has been detected in human plasma, saliva, and urine. Plasma concentrations have been measured at approximately 200 ng/mL in young adults, with levels declining significantly with age — a pattern that has driven much of the research interest in this compound as a model for studying age-related changes in extracellular matrix signaling.

Research Applications

In-vitro studies have investigated GHK-Cu's interactions with several biological pathways:

Extracellular matrix studies: Cell culture research has examined GHK-Cu's effects on collagen synthesis (types I and III), elastin production, and glycosaminoglycan accumulation in fibroblast models.

Gene expression profiling: Broad gene expression studies using cDNA microarrays have identified over 4,000 genes modulated by GHK-Cu in fibroblast cultures, with significant representation in ECM remodeling, antioxidant response, and DNA repair pathways.

Copper delivery mechanism: GHK-Cu serves as a model compound for studying copper ion transport and bioavailability in cellular systems, as copper is a required cofactor for lysyl oxidase, superoxide dismutase, and cytochrome c oxidase.

GHK-Cu vs AHK-Cu

AHK-Cu (alanine-histidine-lysine copper) is a structural analog with alanine substituted for glycine at position 1. Both compounds chelate copper(II) through similar coordination geometry. Comparative binding affinity studies examine whether the glycine-to-alanine substitution affects copper complex stability or cellular uptake in fibroblast models.
